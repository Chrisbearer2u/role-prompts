1.  Write a prompt asking the same question (e.g., "What is climate change?").

        Prompt: "What is climate change?"

2.  Add role instructions for at least 3 personas, such as:

        A.  Scientist → technical explanation:

            Prompt: "Act as a scientist and give a technical explanation on climate change"

            Model Output:

                "Climate change refers to long-term alterations in Earth’s climate system, particularly the sustained increase in global mean surface temperature observed since the late 19th century. From a scientific standpoint, modern climate change is a thermodynamically well-understood consequence of radiative forcing caused primarily by anthropogenic greenhouse gas emissions. The evidence base includes:

                        . Atmospheric measurements

                        . Isotopic chemistry

                        . Satellite observations

                        . Paleoclimate records

                        . Physically consistent climate models

                The core mechanisms are grounded in established physics (radiative transfer, fluid dynamics, thermodynamics), making anthropogenic climate change one of the most robustly supported findings in Earth system science."

        B.  Journalist → informative for the public:

            Prompt: "Act as a journalist and give an informative explanation of climate change to the public"

            Model Output:

                "Climate change refers to long-term shifts in Earth’s temperatures and weather patterns. While the planet’s climate has changed naturally over millions of years, scientists agree that the rapid warming observed since the late 19th century is largely driven by human activity. Climate change is one of the defining issues of our time. The scientific consensus is clear: human activity is driving unprecedented warming. The scale of the challenge is immense, but so is the capacity for innovation and collective action. The decisions made today will shape the planet for generations to come. NASA and Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change say Earth is getting warmer because gases from burning coal, oil, and gas trap heat in the air, making the planet hotter over time. Climate change has economic, social, and political consequences. It affects agriculture, public health, infrastructure, and biodiversity. Vulnerable communities, particularly in low-income regions, are often hit hardest, despite contributing least to global emissions.
                
                What Is Causing Climate Change:
                    
                    The main drivers include:

                        . Burning fossil fuels for electricity, transportation, and industry

                        . Deforestation, which reduces the number of trees that absorb CO₂

                        . Agriculture and livestock, which release methane

                        . Industrial processes and waste management
                        
                The Impacts We Are Seeing:

                Climate change is not a distant threat—it is already affecting communities worldwide. Among the most visible consequences:

                        . Rising sea levels (Due to melting glaciers and thermal expansion of seawater)

                        . More extreme weather events (Including heatwaves, floods, droughts, and stronger storms)

                        . Wildfires (Becoming more frequent and intense)

                        . Ocean warming and acidification (affecting marine ecosystems)

                        . Threats to food and water security
                        
                What Can Be Done?

                Addressing climate change involves both mitigation (reducing emissions) and adaptation (preparing for impacts). Key strategies include:

                        . Transitioning to renewable energy sources such as solar and wind

                        . Improving energy efficiency

                        . Protecting and restoring forests

                        . Electrifying transportation

                        . Investing in climate-resilient infrastructure

                    Individuals also play a role through lifestyle choices—reducing energy use, supporting sustainable policies, and making environmentally conscious purchasing decisions."

        C.  Critic → skeptical tone:

            Prompt: "Act as a critic and give a critic explanation of climate change"

            Model Output:

                  "Climate change is a serious scientific issue, but it should not be treated as unquestionable doctrine. While institutions like the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change provide important research, their projections rely on models with uncertainties. Earth’s climate has naturally varied over history, and policies shaped by agreements such as the Paris Agreement carry political and economic implications. Critical scrutiny ensures science remains open, balanced, and free from alarmism or dogmatic enforcement.

                  Important Points:

                        . Science involves probabilities and uncertainties, not absolute certainty

                        . Climate variability existed before industrialization

                        . Models depend on assumptions and long-term projections

                        . Climate policy can carry political and economic incentives

                        . Alarmist rhetoric may weaken credibility

                        . Energy transitions must consider economic stability

                        . Scientific consensus should not silence debate

                  A climate-change critic (not necessarily a denialist) typically argues:

                        . Human influence exists but magnitude and catastrophe claims may be overstated.

                        . Uncertainty deserves more transparent discussion.

                        . Policy responses may carry political or economic motivations beyond environmental protection.

                        . Debate should remain open and evidence-based."

3.  Compare how the answers differ.

        "When a scientist speaks in the first person, the explanation of climate change is technical, precise, and grounded in empirical data. They might reference atmospheric Carbon dioxide (CO₂) concentrations, radiative forcing, and coupled ocean–atmosphere models, citing institutions such as Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change and datasets from NASA. The tone emphasizes methodology, uncertainty ranges, and peer-reviewed findings. Statements often include quantified projections and controlled language, such as confidence intervals or statistical significance. The focus is on mechanisms—greenhouse gas absorption spectra, feedback loops, and anthropogenic emissions—rather than emotional appeal or political framing.

        A journalist, by contrast, presents climate change in an accessible, informative manner aimed at a broad audience. The explanation translates scientific findings into everyday language, often referencing reports from the World Meteorological Organization or agreements like the Paris Agreement. The tone balances clarity with urgency, highlighting real-world impacts such as extreme weather events, sea-level rise, and economic costs. Human stories, community experiences, and policy debates are woven into the narrative. Technical jargon is simplified, and the emphasis shifts toward relevance, accountability, and societal consequences.

        A critic’s explanation differs by focusing on interpretation, ideology, or perceived shortcomings in climate discourse. Rather than detailing greenhouse gas chemistry, the critic may question policy effectiveness, media framing, or institutional credibility, sometimes referencing activism around figures like Greta Thunberg. The tone can be skeptical, analytical, or oppositional, emphasizing contradictions, uncertainties, or economic trade-offs. The argument may scrutinize regulatory costs, political motivations, or communication strategies. Unlike the scientist’s technical precision or the journalist’s informative clarity, the critic foregrounds evaluation, judgment, and rhetorical analysis over explanatory depth."
